# DNA and RNA Overview

## The Central Dogma

Genetic information flows in one direction:

$$\text{DNA} \xrightarrow{\text{transcription}} \text{RNA} \xrightarrow{\text{translation}} \text{Protein}$$

## DNA Structure

DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid) is a double helix composed of two antiparallel strands. Each strand is a polymer of **nucleotides**, each containing:

1. A deoxyribose sugar
2. A phosphate group
3. A nitrogenous base

### Base Pairing Rules

Bases pair via hydrogen bonds across the two strands:

| Base | Pairs With | Bonds |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Adenine (A) | Thymine (T) | 2 |
| Guanine (G) | Cytosine (C) | 3 |

## RNA

RNA (Ribonucleic acid) differs from DNA in three ways:

- Uses **ribose** sugar (not deoxyribose)
- Is **single-stranded**
- Contains **Uracil (U)** instead of Thymine (T)

### Types of RNA

| Type | Role |
|------|------|
| mRNA (messenger) | Carries the genetic message from DNA to ribosome |
| tRNA (transfer) | Brings amino acids during translation |
| rRNA (ribosomal) | Structural component of ribosomes |

## Transcription

During transcription, RNA polymerase reads the **template strand** of DNA (3'→5') and synthesises a complementary **mRNA** strand (5'→3').

## Translation

Ribosomes read mRNA in triplets called **codons** (e.g. `AUG` = start / methionine). Each codon specifies one amino acid. Translation ends when a stop codon (`UAA`, `UAG`, or `UGA`) is reached.
